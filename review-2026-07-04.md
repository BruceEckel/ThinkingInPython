# Book Review: Suggested Improvements

Full read-through of all 40 Markdown chapters, 2026-07-04. Ordered by impact.
Subtractions are marked **[cut]**, additions **[add]**, fixes **[fix]**.

## High priority

1. **[add] Finish Chapter 19 (Performance).** It is visibly unfinished: seven
   `[[...]]` placeholders (`timeit`, idiomatic Python, lazy evaluation,
   caching, NumPy, Numba, GIL/free threading, choosing a strategy), an empty
   "Converting a Slow Function to Rust" section, and untyped examples
   (`slots.py`, `grade()`) that violate the book's own post-ch-8 standard.
   If it can't be finished soon, consider pulling it from the published site
   until it is — it's the only chapter that reads as a draft.

2. **[cut] Remove "Functions" and "Handling Errors" from Chapter 2 (Tour).**
   The Tour's own opening says it covers "syntax and the scalar types here,"
   with functions and control flow "in the chapters after it" — then it
   teaches both anyway. The Functions section duplicates ch. 5's opening, and
   Handling Errors duplicates ch. 4's "Errors and Exceptions" almost
   point-for-point (both introduce `try`/`except`/`as`/`raise`/`finally`).
   Cutting both sections (~85 lines) makes the Tour match its stated scope and
   removes the book's largest redundancy.

3. **[fix] The asyncio promise is never kept.** Ch. 19 and ch. 31 both say
   "the `asyncio` mechanics (`async def`, `await`, `gather`, `run`) are
   covered in [Simulation](38)" — but ch. 38 uses them without ever teaching
   them. Either add a short primer section to ch. 38 (or a standalone
   mini-chapter), or soften both cross-references to "used in."

4. **[fix] Misplaced exercises: Surrogate vs. State Machines.** Ch. 25
   (Surrogate) has no exercises; ch. 26's exercises 1, 2, and 5 are Proxy
   exercises that even carry an apologetic note pointing back to ch. 25.
   Move them to a new Exercises section in ch. 25.

5. **[add] Exercise coverage is inconsistent in Parts II–III.** Part I having
   none reads as deliberate, but within the later parts these chapters lack
   exercises while all their neighbors have them: 16 (Context Managers),
   17 (Comprehensions), 18 (Metaprogramming), 20 (Rethinking Objects),
   32 (Multiple Dispatching), 39 (Functional Programming).

## Medium priority

6. **[cut] Chapter 21's "candidates" list.** The list of structural principles
   (Gathering, Localization, Barrier/fence, Mirror, Shadow...) is 2008-era
   material: undefined one-word entries never referenced again anywhere in the
   book. Cut it, or compress to a sentence. Relatedly, the claim "This book
   attempts to present the patterns based on their structure instead of the
   categories presented in GoF" no longer matches Part III's actual
   organization — revise or delete the claim.

7. **[cut/fix] Chapter 26 mousetrap examples.** Three problems:
   - `sys.path += ["..", "../mouse"]` plus `# type: ignore` on the import is
     exactly the kind of hack the rest of the book teaches readers to avoid.
     Restructure so the imports are clean, or acknowledge the hack in prose.
   - mousetrap2's lazy-initialized transition tables solve a
     "cyclic initialization" problem that module-level tables (assigned after
     the classes exist) don't have — a Java-ism worth replacing or calling out.
   - The two identical 36-line outputs appear in full twice (~70 lines).
     Truncate the second, or collapse mousetrap1/2 into one example and get to
     the (excellent) table-driven version faster. This is the book's
     second-longest chapter and much of it is repeated output.

8. **[cut] Singleton material duplicated between chs. 18 and 23.** Ch. 18
   shows the metaclass `__call__` singleton *and* the decorator singleton
   *and* the module observation; ch. 23 covers all three again. Keep the
   `__call__` interception demo in ch. 18 (it illustrates metamethods) and
   replace the decorator version with a one-line pointer to ch. 23.

9. **[cut] Chapter 39 re-teaches earlier chapters.** Lambdas (ch. 5),
   comprehensions/declarative (ch. 17), pattern matching (ch. 13), and
   functional error handling (ch. 14) each get a full re-introduction. The
   chapter's real contributions — purity, closures, partials, composition,
   referential transparency, the assurance spectrum, Hypothesis — are strong;
   tightening the recap sections to a paragraph plus cross-reference would
   shorten the chapter by a quarter and sharpen its argument.
   Also: ch. 11 (Testing) never mentions property-based testing; add a
   forward reference to ch. 39's Hypothesis section.

10. **[fix] Chapter 2 teaches `%`-formatting first.** The printf section
    precedes f-strings and is presented neutrally ("Python uses C's printf()
    string substitution syntax"). Lead with f-strings, then demote `%` to a
    short "you'll see this in older code" note — matching how the typing
    chapter handles legacy spellings.

11. **[fix] "Naming Conventions"/"File Names" placement.** These sit at the
    end of the Tour, after exceptions — an odd home for style rules. The File
    Names material (importability, shadowing stdlib modules, test naming)
    belongs naturally in ch. 6 (Modules and Packages).

## Small / consistency

12. **[fix] Intro claims "uses type hints throughout,"** but chapters 2–7 are
    deliberately unannotated until ch. 8 introduces typing. Add a sentence to
    "The Examples": early chapters omit hints until Static Typing introduces
    them.

13. **[fix] Part boundary mismatch.** The Introduction describes the
    question-OOP chapter (20) as ending *Techniques*, but `build_site.py`'s
    `PARTS` starts Part III *Patterns* at 20. Align one with the other.

14. **[fix] Data files in `python` fences.** `trash.dat` (ch. 37) and
    `mouse/mouse_moves.txt` (ch. 26) are fenced as ```` ```python ````, while
    `amaze.txt` (ch. 38) uses ```` ```text ````. Wrong highlighting for data;
    standardize on `text` if the extractor allows it.

15. **[fix] Duplicate example filename** `visitor_singledispatch.py` exists in
    both ch. 33 (flowers) and ch. 37 (trash) with different contents. Fine on
    disk, confusing in a book; rename ch. 37's to e.g. `recycling_note.py`.

16. **[fix] Figures.** Only four figures survive, all 2008-era GIFs/PNGs
    (`listComprehensions`, `idMatrix`, `surrogate`, `stateMachine`).
    `resources/images/` still holds unused diagrams (`decorator*`,
    `coffeeExplosion`, `trashSorter1–3`, `trashVisitor`) — the decorator and
    trash-sorter diagrams would genuinely help chs. 15 and 37. Either
    regenerate the set as crisp SVGs (Claude can do this) or cut the remaining
    four; the current mix looks dated next to the modernized text.

17. **[fix] Aging constants.** Ch. 12's `Year` validates `n <= 2026` — it will
    start rejecting the current year in 2027. Ch. 3's "no frozen dict until
    Python 3.15" is correct (PEP 814, verified), but plan to reword once 3.15
    ships in October.

18. **[fix] Ch. 7 "Composing Methods with `import`"** presents an unusual
    idiom (importing a function into a class body) with no caveat. Add a
    sentence saying it's a curiosity readers will rarely want, or cut it.

19. **[fix] Ch. 6 PYTHONPATH section** could name the modern practice
    concretely: `uv sync` / editable installs, since the book already
    standardizes on uv.

20. **[cut?] Chapter 22 (Data Transfer Objects)** is 128 lines and mostly says
    "use `SimpleNamespace`/dataclass/NamedTuple." It works as a short palate
    cleanser opening Part III, but it could also fold into ch. 12 or ch. 21 if
    you want one fewer chapter.

## What's in good shape

Chapters 12–16, 20, 34–37 are the strongest in the book — clear arguments,
modern Python, tests woven in. The recurring "here's the GoF form, here's what
Python dissolves it into" structure lands consistently, and the
Composite/Interpreter and Memento chapters are the best versions of that move.
The Pattern Catalog appendix is a genuinely useful addition.
